Lecture 1 - How to start a startup

Sam Altman How to start a startup

1. idea

  • Wait to start a startup until you come up with an idea you feel compelled to explore.(This is also the way to chose in multiple ideas.)
Another way to look at this, is the most company often mission oriented. It is difficult to get large groups of people to the extreme levels of focus and productivity that you need, for a startup to be successful, unless the company feels like an important mission. And it is usually really hard to get that without a really good founding idea.
  • Good startup idea usually seems terrible at first.
If they sound really good, it will be too many people working on them. You want an idea to turn monopoly, but you can't get monopoly in a big market. It has too many competitors. The hard part is that this is a very find line. There's right on one side of it , and crazy on the other. When most people think your idea is bad, you should be happy about that. It means they won't compete with you. It is also the reason why it is not usually dangerous to tell people about your idea. The truly good ideas don't sound like they're worth stealing. You wanna sound crazy, but you wanna actually be right. Unpopular but right is what you're going for.
  • Don't just think about the growth of the market. Think about the growth of market.
  • Ask yourself: Why now? Why it couldn't be done two years ago and why will two years later will be too late.
  • In general, it is best if you're building something that you yourself need. You'll understand it much better than if you have to understand it by talking to a customer to build a very first version.

Another counter-intuitive about good startup ideas is that

  • they are almost always very easy too explain and very easy too understand.




2. Product


  • Everything (e.g. raising money, hiring) will be significant easily if you first have a great product.

*Step.1*


  • Build something that users love.
  • Talk to users

Find a small group of user and make them really love what you're doing.

It's better to build something that a small number of users love than a large number of users like


One way to know that this is working is that you'll get growth by word of mouth. (organic growth)

If you don't have some early organic growth, then your product probably isn't good enough yet.


The goal: Get the small group of them to love you. Understand that group extremely well. Get extremely close to them.


The Feedback circle.

You should make this feedback loop as tight as possible.


Metrics: be honest at the growth number.

This make you know if you're on the right direction.

Startup live on growth. It's the indicator of a great product
